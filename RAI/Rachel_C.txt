[05:03 pm] Coach:
    LH surges can vary a lot between people and even from cycle to cycle. Some surges are sharp and short, others are lower and more gradual — both patterns are commonly seen and can still successfully trigger ovulation.
    
    Research supports this wide variability. One study found that while the average LH level during a surge was 44.6 mIU/mL, ovulation still occurred with LH levels as low as 6.5 mIU/mL and as high as 101 mIU/mL. This shows that there isn’t a single “required” LH number for ovulation. Study - https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272175944_Development_of_the_first_urinary_reproductive_hormone_ranges_referenced_to_independently_determined_ovulation_day
    
    Rather than the absolute LH number, the sequence is key:
    estrogen rose → LH increased and peaked → PdG rose afterward.
    Your chart follows that pattern clearly.

[11:52 am] Coach:
    🌷 What your chart shows
    • E3G climbs steadily through the fertile window
    • A strong peak precedes the LH surge around CD 16–17
    • Levels fall after ovulation and then fluctuate mildly in the luteal phase
    
    🌼 Why estrogen can look “high”
    Estrogen is produced by the dominant follicle as it matures. A strong follicle can produce higher estrogen, which helps:
    • thicken the uterine lining
    • improve cervical mucus quality
    • signal the brain to trigger the LH surge
    
    🌻 What’s considered common
    Urinary E3G varies widely between individuals and cycles. Peaks can differ based on:
    • follicle activity
    • hydration and urine concentration
    • metabolism and timing of testing
    • normal cycle-to-cycle variability
    
    What matters more than the absolute number is the pattern: rise → peak → LH surge → rise → drop.
    
    🌸 When to look closer
    Consider medical review if you feel your levels are off the charts or if estrogen stays persistently elevated without ovulation or cycles become irregular. That pattern is not visible here currently.
