[08:55 pm] Coach:
    I can see why this feels conflicting. Trigger cycles don’t follow the same hormone rules as natural cycles, so patterns can look confusing if they’re read the same way.
    
    For ease of calculation, Inito marks ovulation as the day after the latest LH peak when a PdG rise follows that surge. This is a standardized way of labeling data, not a claim about the exact biological moment ovulation occurred.
    
    In your case, you received a trigger shot on CD 8, which causes LH to rise and remain elevated for several days. Instead of a sharp, single surge, this creates a plateau-style LH pattern, making it difficult to pinpoint ovulation using LH alone.
    
    Biologically, ovulation typically occurs 24–36 hours after the LH surge (and about 8–20 hours after the LH peak). For IUI, clinics intentionally time the procedure 24–36 hours after the surge so sperm and egg overlap.
    
    Because this surge was trigger-induced, the LH rise seen on CD 9 is the true ovulatory signal, and that is what initiated ovulation. The later LH activity reflects lingering effects of the trigger shot rather than a second ovulation attempt.
    
    It’s also important to note that Inito hasn’t been clinically tested for use in medicated or trigger cycles, so we don’t yet have enough data to reliably interpret medication-driven hormone patterns the same way we do natural cycles. In these cases, Inito can show hormone trends, but clinical timing and outcomes are best guided by your care team.
    
    Your IUI was timed to the biologically relevant window — the chart just reflects how trigger cycles behave hormonally.
    
    If you’d like to dig deeper into timing after an LH surge, this blog explains it well:
    https://blog.inito.com/when-to-inseminate-after-lh-surge/
