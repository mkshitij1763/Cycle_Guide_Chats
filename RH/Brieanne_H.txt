    Hi there, this is my first month charting right after my period ended as I didn’t have my monitor early enough last cycle. What is a normal estrogen pattern? Mine was climbing then it severely dropped.
----------------------------------------

[09:13 pm] Coach:
    Hey Brie, it’s typical to see some fluctuations in estrogen levels.
    
    Estrogen is released in pulses as the dominant follicle develops, so it can rise and dip throughout your cycle. A sudden drop can feel concerning, but it can happen as part of natural variation.
    
    Estrogen rises during the follicular phase, about 3-4 days before ovulation, which marks the start of your fertile window. Right now, since you’re just after your period, your body is still establishing its baseline for this cycle. When estrogen rises above your baseline, you’ll start to see a high fertility rating.
    
    It’s also important to remember that levels can vary based on factors like follicle activity, hydration, stress, how hormones are metabolized in urine, and overall lifestyle, so some ups and downs are expected.
    What matters most is the overall trend across the cycles, rather than the values.
    
    If you’d like to understand estrogen patterns in more detail, this blog might be helpful: https://blog.inito.com/estradiol-levels-chart/

[02:33 pm] Coach:
    Hormone trends on Inito, after ovulation is confirmed can’t be used to predict your period or pregnancy. So even if your hormones aren’t trending down in a certain way, it doesn’t reliably indicate either outcome.
    
    It could be that your hCG levels aren’t high enough to be detected yet, or that your body is still in the normal post-ovulation phase.
    
    It's ideally recommended to take a pregnancy test at around 14 DPO or on the first day of your missed period for the most accurate results. Testing too early can lead to a false negative since your hCG levels might not be high enough yet.
    
    If you’d like a deeper understanding of how timing affects pregnancy test accuracy, you can read this blog: https://blog.inito.com/how-long-after-ovulation-can-you-take-a-pregnancy-test/
