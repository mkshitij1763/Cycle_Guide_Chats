[01:11 am] Coach:
    Great! Thank you for taking the time to read through it!
    
    I do want to address your question before about hormone levels. So hormone levels can vary a lot from person to person. They’re influenced by many factors, such as how your body metabolizes hormones, the amount released in each pulse, the phase of your cycle, and lifestyle factors like diet, exercise, and stress.
    
    What really matters is the trend we see in your chart, rather than the absolute numbers. 
    
    For example, studies have found that the average LH level during a surge is 44.6 mIU/mL. However, LH levels could be as low as 6.5 or as high as 101 and still trigger ovulation. Here's the study if you'd like to read more about it: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272175944_Development_of_the_first_urinary_reproductive_hormone_ranges_referenced_to_independently_determined_ovulation_day

[12:21 am] Coach:
    Hey Chieri, I'm so glad it helped! As for your question, EWCM is driven by rising estrogen, not ovulation itself. So while it can signal that your body is gearing up, it doesn’t actually confirm that ovulation happened. That’s where PdG comes in! A steady and consistent rise in PdG over a few days is what helps confirm ovulation. 
    
    EWCM also varies a lot person to person. Only about 15% of women consistently notice it, and even then it’s only about 48 to 76% accurate for pinpointing ovulation. So it makes sense that you might see EWCM even after ovulation sometimes, and it doesn’t necessarily mean things are off.
    
    I love that you’re choosing to trust the process and protect your peace. With Inito, testing after ovulation is completely up to you!

    Hey Chieri, usually, the trend over the next day or two helps give us more clarity, if LH continues to rise, it suggests the surge is still building.
    
    I recommend testing with first morning urine (FMU) as it's the most accurate. A long urine hold ensures consistency and minimizes external factors that can alter hormone levels. 
    
    If you’d like a little more insight on days when you suspect a surge, you could consider testing a second time later in the day. Just make sure to wait at least about 6 hours since your last bathroom break (a urine hold) and try to limit fluids during that window so the sample isn’t diluted. This can sometimes help capture changes that occur later in the day.
